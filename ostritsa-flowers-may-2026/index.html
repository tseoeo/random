9 May 2026 · Saturday · 11 species in flower

A snapshot of what's in bloom today on Bulgaria's oldest dedicated nature reserve, ~45 minutes west of Sofia. Stay on the marked trails — every plant on this page is photographed, never picked.

About the reserve

Поддържан резерват „Острица“ sits on the limestone slopes of Голо бърдо (Bare Hill), between the towns of Pernik and Radomir. Declared in 1943, it's the oldest dedicated nature reserve in Bulgaria — older than the country's national-park system itself. The reason it was protected is simple: it holds an extraordinary concentration of rare plants on just 134 hectares.

Today the reserve protects 453 plant species, including 5 Bulgarian endemics, 32 Balkan endemics, and 23 species in Bulgaria's Red Book. The flagship is the Bulgarian-endemic Urumov's tulip, *Tulipa urumoffii*, which grows on only a handful of limestone hills in the country's west — and Острица is one of them.

Mid-May is peak season. The eleven species below are what hikers, tour operators and botanists are seeing in flower this weekend.

Урумово лале

Tulipa urumoffii

Urumov's tulip

🇧🇬 Bulgarian endemic 📕 Red Book ✅ At peak

The reason this reserve was declared in 1943. Tulipa urumoffii is a Bulgarian endemic — it grows nowhere else in the world, and only on a few limestone hills in the country's west: Острица, Чепън, and parts of Konyavska. The petals are warm yellow with red streaks and blotches, the way a child draws a tulip.

Named for Ivan Urumov, the Bulgarian botanist who described it. In a good year you can find clumps of dozens scattered across the south-facing slopes — but they're tiny (15–25 cm) and easy to walk past unless you're looking down.

Райхенбахова перуника

Iris reichenbachii

Reichenbach's iris

🏔 Balkan endemic ✅ At peak

Look for two colour forms growing side by side on the same slope: a glowing buttery yellow and a smoky dusky-purple. Same species, same population — botanists call this colour polymorphism, and it's particularly striking in this iris.

Named for the 19th-century German botanist H.G. Reichenbach. A Balkan endemic restricted to the mountains of the western Balkans — so what looks like an ordinary roadside iris is actually a regional speciality.

Дребна перуника

Iris pumila

Dwarf iris

🌍 Widespread but special here ✅ At peak

You'll have to crouch — Iris pumila is only 5 to 15 cm tall, so it's lost in the grass unless you're paying attention. The reward is a perfect miniature of a garden iris: same architecture, palm-of-your-hand scale.

One of the few flowers where a single meadow holds purple, blue, yellow and white forms all at once — the plant is genetically promiscuous about colour. Its native range stretches from southeastern Europe across the Eurasian steppes all the way to the Urals.

Пролетен горицвет

Adonis vernalis

Spring pheasant's eye

📕 Red Book ✅ At peak

Glossy yellow flowers the size of a small saucer, sitting ankle-high on a fern of finely divided leaves. The Bulgarian name горицвет literally means "burning flower" — once you see them lit by spring sun on a south-facing slope, you understand why.

Pharmacologically important: the plant produces cardiac glycosides used historically in heart medicine. It's also toxic if eaten, which (along with the strong yellow signal) is its defence against grazers.

Бадемче

Amygdalus nana (= Prunus tenella)

Dwarf wild almond

🌍 Widespread but special here ✅ At peak

A knee-high shrub, blanketed in pink-and-white blossoms that look like a miniature flowering cherry. It is the wild relative of the cultivated almond — the genus and the family are the same, even if the nuts are tiny and not the kind you eat from a bag.

Острица's south-facing limestone is exactly the kind of dry, sunny soil Amygdalus nana evolved for. In peak bloom, an open slope can look like someone has scattered handfuls of pink popcorn across the ground.

Люляк

Syringa vulgaris

Common lilac (wild form)

🌍 Widespread but special here ✅ At peak

Yes — that lilac. The plant in a billion gardens worldwide. But here at Острица you're seeing it where it actually evolved: clinging to limestone slopes in the southern Balkans. Every cultivated garden lilac on the planet descends from these wild Balkan populations.

In Bulgarian poetry the lilac stands in for spring itself — the moment when the country smells of nothing else. Crush a few flowers between your fingers and the perfume is unmistakable.

Бъзов дланокоренник

Dactylorhiza sambucina

Elder-flowered orchid

📕 Red Book ✅ At peak

One species, two completely different colours, side by side in the same population — yellow plants and deep-purple plants standing a few metres apart. Botanists are still arguing over why this colour polymorphism is stable; pollinators, it turns out, can't quite tell them apart.

The species name sambucina means "elder-like," from Sambucus — bend down close and you'll catch a faint, sweet hint of elderflower in the air. A protected species across Europe; in Bulgaria, Red Book.

Тризъбест салеп

Neotinea tridentata (former Orchis tridentata)

Three-toothed orchid

📕 Red Book ✅ At peak

Tightly packed pink-purple flowers gathered into a small pyramid at the top of a 15–25 cm stem. Each individual flower has a three-lobed lower lip — that's the "tridentata" / three-toothed in the name.

Like all native European orchids it depends on a partnership with soil fungi to get going from seed, which is why it can't simply be transplanted to your garden. It has to be where it is, and any flowering plant you see is the visible tip of an underground partnership years in the making.

Обикновен салеп

Anacamptis morio (former Orchis morio)

Green-winged orchid

📕 Red Book ✅ At peak

Look at the side wings of each flower — small purple hoods, but with vivid green veins running through them. That's where the English common name comes from: the "green-winged" orchid. The whole plant is short, only 10 to 20 cm tall, with violet-pink flowers loosely arranged on a slim stem.

Once carpeted hay meadows across Europe; vanishing fast wherever fields are fertilised or ploughed too hard. In a real sense it's an indicator: where Anacamptis morio still grows, the meadow is intact.

Самовилски божур

Paeonia mascula

Male peony / fairy peony

📕 Red Book 🌱 Early peak

Pink-rose petals as broad as your palm, framing a yolk-yellow bouquet of stamens. The Bulgarian name самовилски means "of the самодиви" — the forest fairies of Slavic folklore. Tradition says picking a fairy peony brings bad luck on the picker, which (very conveniently) is also exactly what helps the population survive.

The Острица population is small but real. Most plants are still tight buds today; you may catch the very first opening flowers if you walk slowly and look in shaded oak edges.

Сръбско звънче

Edraianthus serbicus

Serbian bellflower

🏔 Balkan endemic 🌸 Just opening

A tight cushion of grass-like leaves with little upward-facing purple bells clustered at the centre — like a tiny posy you didn't have to arrange. Each bell is no bigger than the tip of your finger, in the family Campanulaceae (the bellflowers).

A Balkan endemic that hugs the ground on rocky limestone, exactly the substrate Острица is built on. The first photo on this card was actually taken on Голо бърдо — same hill you're walking on. Most plants are still budding in early May; the show peaks in late May to early June.

🌿 Stay on marked trails. Photograph only — picking plants is forbidden by law inside the reserve.
